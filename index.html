A Scientific Comparison of Cemented and Cementless TKR

Introduction

Total knee arthroplasty (TKA), also known as total knee replacement surgery (TKR), is a surgical procedure performed to alleviate chronic knee pain and restore joint mobility in patients with severe arthritis, osteonecrosis, or other degenerative joint diseases. Surgery replacement provides significant pain relief as well as restored mobility to the joint while preventing further damage and strain to adjacent joints, such as the hips.

The TKA procedure involves removing damaged cartilage and bone from the knee joint and replacing them with artificial components, usually made from metal alloys and durable plastics to simulate the functions of a healthy knee. Although TKA can provide long-term relief, the artificial implants wear over time and may require revision, especially in younger and more active patients. Despite this, TKA remains one of the most effective treatments for severe knee degeneration, relieving symptoms and restoring function when conservative treatments fail.

Globally, TKA has become one of the most common orthopedic procedures, with over 100,000 operations performed annually in the UK alone. Cemented fixation is the predominant method, used in approximately 95% of primary TKR in the UK and 68% in Australia, where cementless fixation accounts for only 11% of cases. However, advances in implant design and manufacturing technology have renewed interest in cementless fixation. This method offers potential advantages, such as shorter surgical times and the possibility of long-lasting biological fixation, making it a promising option for certain patient populations.

Main Body

Cementless Fixation

This method involves press-fitting the metal parts that attach to the femur and tibia. The components are made with a porous surface to allow bone growth, ensuring a tight fit around the implants. However, bone cement is still used to fix the patella, as the cementless method has proven ineffective in this area.

Advantages

  • Does not have drawbacks associated with bone cement, such as cracking or loosening, which could lead to osteolysis.
  • Better suited for younger, more active patients with healthier bones capable of generating new bone at the implant interface.
  • Shorter surgical times, as the surgeon does not need to wait for the cement to set.
  • Simpler revision surgeries due to intact bone around the implant and the absence of cement removal.

Disadvantages

  • Relies on bone growth for stability, resulting in longer recovery times with possible discomfort and instability.
  • Requires excellent bone health, typically found in younger patients.
  • Demands a high degree of surgical precision for proper bone removal and implant placement to avoid surgical failure.
  • Lacks long-term outcome data, as it is a relatively recent approach.
  • Higher cost due to specialized materials and implant designs promoting bone growth.

Cemented Fixation

In this method, the surgeon drills a hole in the tibia slightly larger than the implant, leaving space to apply bone cement—a long-lasting, fast-hardening mixture. The implant does not require a porous surface, as the bone cement ensures proper fixation. As with cementless methods, the patella is fixed with bone cement.

Advantages

  • Extensively researched, with durability lasting around 10–20 years without revision.
  • Provides immediate implant stability, reducing recovery time.
  • Cement may contain antibiotics, lowering the risk of infection.
  • Less manipulation of the knee's interfaces during surgery, reducing blood loss and improving postoperative outcomes.
  • Well-suited for patients with poor bone quality, typically older adults.

Disadvantages

  • Cement may break or flake, leading to inflammation and osteolysis.
  • The hardening process generates heat, potentially damaging surrounding bone tissue.
  • Cement durability depends on surgeon expertise, as proper mixing and application are crucial.
  • Less suitable for younger, active patients, as physical activity accelerates cement deterioration.
  • Revision surgeries are more complicated due to the need to remove the cement and address weakened bone stock.

Conclusion

The main driver for selecting a cemented or cementless TKA procedure lies in the patient’s age, which is closely related to overall health and bone quality. The skill and familiarity of the surgeon with the procedure also play a significant role.

Cementless TKA: Best suited for younger, active patients with excellent bone quality. It relies on biological fixation, offering potential for greater longevity and more straightforward revision surgery. The procedure requires shorter surgical time but demands high surgical precision and may result in initial instability. It is costly, and long-term data on outcomes is still limited.

Cemented TKA: Ideal for older patients or those with poor bone quality, as it provides immediate stability through bone cement. Recovery is quicker, and infection risk is reduced due to antibiotic-laden cement. However, the cement can wear or fracture over time, leading to loosening and potential need for complex revision surgeries.
