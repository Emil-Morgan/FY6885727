A Scientific Comparison of Cemented and Cementless TKR

Introduction

Total knee arthroplasty (TKA), also known as total knee replacement surgery (TKR), is a surgical procedure performed to alleviate chronic knee pain and restore joint mobility in patients with severe arthritis, osteonecrosis, or other degenerative joint diseases. Surgery replacement will provide significant pain relief as well as return of mobility to the joint and prevent further damage and strain to spread to other joints such as the hips.

The TKA procedure consists of removing damaged cartilage and bone from the knee joint and replacing them with artificial components, usually made from metal alloys and durable plastics to simulate the functions of a healthy knee. Although TKA can provide long-term relief, the artificial implants wear over time and need revision, especially in younger and more active patients, who may need revision surgery in the future.

TKA is still one of the most effective treatments for severe knee degeneration, by relieving symptoms and restoring function when conservative treatments have failed. Globally, TKA has become one of the most common orthopaedic procedures, with over 100,000 operations performed annually in the UK alone. Cemented fixation is the predominant method, used in approximately 95% of primary TKR in the UK and 68% in Australia, where cementless fixation accounts for only 11% of cases. However, advances in implant design and manufacturing technology have renewed interest in cementless fixation. This method offers potential advantages, such as shorter surgical times and the possibility of long-lasting biological fixation, making it a promising possibility for certain patient populations.

Main Body

During TKA, the metal parts of the prosthesis are fixed with two different methods:

Cementless Fixation

Cementless fixation is done by press fitting the metal parts that attach to the femur and tibia. The components are made specifically with a porous surface to allow the bone to grow and attach itself to the components, and the bones are shaped in way to ensure a tight fit around the implants. However, bone cement is used to fix the patella in place because the cementless method has been shown to be ineffective for this purpose.

  • Advantages:
    • Avoids issues with cement, such as cracking and loosening, which can lead to osteolysis.
    • More suitable for younger, active patients with healthy bone stock, reducing the risk of implant loosening.
    • Shorter surgery times, as there is no need to wait for cement to set.
    • Simpler revisions, as bone around the implant remains intact and no cement removal is required.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Implant stability takes longer due to reliance on bone growth, leading to potential discomfort and instability during recovery.
    • Requires good bone health, typically found in younger patients.
    • High surgical precision is required for proper implant placement; gaps between implant and bone can cause failure.
    • Limited long-term data on outcomes, as this method is relatively new.
    • Cementless implants tend to be more expensive due to specialized materials and designs.

Cemented Fixation

The surgeon must drill a hole in the tibia slightly larger than the implant, to leave space in between the implant and the bone to apply a product called bone cement, a mixture that is long lasting as well as fast hardening. The implants are different to the cementless one because there is no need for the porous surface, as the bone cement fixes the implant properly without it. And as mentioned above, the patella is fixed to the knee with bone cement in both cases.

  • Advantages:
    • Well-established with proven durability of 10–20 years without needing revision surgery.
    • Immediate implant stability results in faster recovery.
    • Cement often contains antibiotics, lowering infection risk.
    • Requires less manipulation during surgery, leading to less blood loss and better range of motion post-surgery.
    • Ideal for patients with poor bone quality, such as older adults, who have reduced capacity for bone growth at the implant interface.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Cement can crack or flake, causing inflammation, osteolysis, and loosening of the prosthesis.
    • Cement wear is faster in younger, more active patients, making it less suitable for them.
    • The cement hardening process generates heat, which can damage surrounding bone tissue.
    • Cement durability depends on the surgeon’s skill, and improper application can affect long-term outcomes.
    • Revision surgeries are more complex, requiring cement removal and often a bone graft due to weakened bone stock.

Comparison Table

Feature Cemented TKA Cementless TKA
Target Patient Older patients or those with poor bone quality Younger, active patients with excellent bone quality
Stability Immediate stability through bone cement Relies on biological fixation (longer recovery time)
Recovery Faster recovery due to immediate fixation Potential for initial instability
Longevity May deteriorate over time (cement wear or fracture) Potentially greater longevity with successful bone growth
Cost More affordable due to established methods Higher cost due to advanced materials and designs
Revision Surgery More complex due to cement removal Simpler revisions as no cement is involved
Suitability Best for low-activity individuals Best for active individuals with good bone quality
Infection Risk Reduced due to antibiotic-laden cement Comparable but without cement-based antibiotics

Conclusion

The main driver for selecting cement or cementless TKA procedure main differences is the age of the patient, which in turn is related to the patient’s overall health and bone quality. The skill and familiarity of the surgeon with the procedure is also a factor. Cementless TKA: Best suited for younger, active patients with excellent bone quality. It relies on biological fixation, offering potential for greater longevity and more straight forward revision surgery. The procedure requires shorter surgical time but demands high surgical precision and may result in first instability. It is costly, and long-term data on outcomes is still limited. Cemented TKA: Ideal for older patients or those with poor bone quality, as it provides immediate stability through bone cement. Recovery is quicker, and infection risk is reduced due to antibiotic-laden cement. However, the cement can wear or fracture over time, leading to loosening and potential need for complex revision surgeries.
